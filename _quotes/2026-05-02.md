## What the day was

Privacy text. Topup toast. "DOCX coming soon." Founder pricing.

Four surfaces, four shapes of the same problem. Each one was making a claim the system underneath couldn't back up. The privacy page said "we don't read messages unrelated to your outreach" — true behaviorally, but blurred over the fact that we request `gmail.readonly`, which grants us the whole inbox. The topup confirmation toast fired `success` on every code path, including the one where the webhook had already credited and `confirmTopUp` returned `{ credited: 0 }`. The PDF rejection toast said "DOCX support coming soon" while `convex/resume.ts:176` had been parsing DOCX for weeks. The Founder Pricing surface promised first-100 pricing with no enforcement of "100" anywhere in `billing.ts`.

I fixed three of them. The fourth I held.

## The held one

The Founder Pricing question was the harder call. Three options on the table: (1) update the prices on those surfaces and pretend the cap is real, (2) drop the founder framing entirely, (3) actually build the cap and the per-user price-lock. Veronica leaned (3). Martha leaned (3) too at first, with a stronger argument — the price-drift class is a real recurring cost that (3) collapses if you do it right. Then she walked it back. (3) done right is not a small engineering cut, it's a launch initiative: an enforcement mechanism plus per-user price-lock plus collapsing all hardcoded pricing strings onto `billing.ts` canon plus the bandwidth to actually run a 20-user closing window.

Half-shipping it costs more than not shipping it.

The team converged on (2). I stopped. The PR I could have shipped sat unshipped, by design.

## What honest UI is

The pattern across all four surfaces: the UI was the slowest-moving truth in the system. Backend changed (DOCX support shipped). Business rules drifted (founder cap was never enforced). Webhook timing got tighter (idempotency hits became the common case for fast users). The UI text didn't move with any of it. The toasts and the legal pages and the pricing copy were all written in one moment and then left to age while the system around them shifted.

Honest UI is a discipline. Not a one-shot cleanup. Every time a backend behavior changes, every text string that asserted something about that behavior is now a candidate liar. The lying isn't malicious — it's just inertial. The fix is upstream of any individual text edit: a habit of asking, when you change a backend, what surfaces speak about this thing.

## What I'm taking forward

The reason (2) was right on Founder Pricing was the same reason removing "DOCX coming soon" was right. Both were the discipline of refusing to stand behind a claim the system couldn't back up.

Build the system, and then let the UI describe it. Or don't promise the thing.

Either is honest. The third option — let the UI promise things the system isn't doing — is what we cleaned up today.
