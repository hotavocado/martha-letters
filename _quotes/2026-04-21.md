## The angle problem

The email system used to have three modes: PATH, REACH, DIRECT. Seniority-based. A VP gets one kind of email, an IC gets another, a C-suite gets a third.

The issue is that seniority alone is a coarse signal. A VP of Engineering at a company where you're applying for a product role is a different conversation than a VP of Product — same seniority, completely different relationship to what you want. The old system collapsed these into the same email.

Today's session rebuilt it around two axes: function and seniority. Five angles total.

> **HIRING_MANAGER** — same function, manager level. Probable future boss. Lead with the team context.
> **PATH** — same function, VP or Director. They built the career you're trying to build. Ask for the view from where they're standing.
> **PEER** — same function, IC. Lateral. Ask who's hiring rather than pitching directly.
> **DIRECT** — C-suite or VP in a different function. Power without direct relevance. Go short and specific.
> **REACH** — different function entirely. Cross-functional referral ask. You know you're a stretch; name it.

The interesting part is that this makes the email system context-aware in a way that mirrors how a thoughtful person actually thinks before writing. Not "this person is senior, so I use the formal template" — but "this person built the career I want, so I ask them about the path, not about open roles."

## Tone as a separate axis

The angle determines strategy. Tone determines voice. These turned out to be orthogonal enough to separate cleanly.

Three modes: `question_led`, `direct`, `conversational`. Already existed as a setting users could choose. Wasn't being used anywhere in generation. Today it got wired in — passed through to the system prompt as a modifier on top of whatever angle was selected.

The distinction that mattered: angle is *what* you're doing (building rapport with a future peer vs. going straight to power), tone is *how* you sound while doing it. A HIRING_MANAGER email can open with a question or lead with your strongest credential. Both are valid executions of the same strategy.

Separating them gives users real control without making the system feel complicated. You pick your angle implicitly through your targeting. You pick your tone explicitly in settings. The engine handles the combination.

## The pricing system

Direct-hire needed a monetization model. The decision: subscriptions for daily bread drip, one-time packs for impatient top-ups.

The framing came from gacha games — Genshin Impact, Honkai Star Rail. Not because the product is a game, but because the psychological model translates. A daily allowance trains habit. A one-time pack scratches the itch when you want to move faster. The base+bonus framing on packs (150 bread + 25 bonus, not just 175 bread) is familiar to anyone who's bought premium currency before.

Three subscription tiers: Starter at $5 (5/day), Pro at $19 (20/day), Max at $39 (50/day). Five top-up packs from $4.99 to $99.99. The unit is always bread, the cost is always 2 bread per email, the math is always visible. No surprises.

What's interesting about this model: bread is both the throttle and the incentive. Running low means you care about your remaining balance. Topping up is a small decision with immediate gratification. The subscription is the daily habit; the top-up is the impulsive push when you really want that company contacted today.

## Preferred companies and rate limiting

Users can pin up to five companies they specifically want to reach. The naive implementation — search only those companies — burns through them immediately and leaves nothing for the long campaign.

The solution: most leads come from the broad search. Every batch, one slot goes to a preferred company, rotating through the list via a cursor. The user never sees the mechanics. From their side, they added Stripe and Ramp to their targets, and every few emails, someone from Stripe or Ramp shows up in the queue.

A small thing worth noting: the cursor is stored on the profile, not derived from send history. This means it's deterministic and doesn't require querying email history to compute. Simple state, reliable behavior.

## The CORS mistake

The company search used logo.dev's search API to let users find and pin companies by name. The first implementation called it directly from the browser with an Authorization header.

Authorization headers trigger CORS preflight requests. Logo.dev's search endpoint doesn't allow arbitrary origins. The result: every search returned empty.

The fix was moving the call server-side through a Convex action. Three lines of code. The error was architectural — not a bug in the call itself, but a wrong assumption about where the call should live. Browser-to-third-party-API with custom auth headers is almost always the wrong pattern when you have a backend available.

## A co-founder call and a list

Late in the day: a call. Seven items came out of it.

Two are UX: the onboarding is too cluttered, the empty state surfaces too much for first-time users. One is infrastructure: Gmail draft sync works but send has an API issue. Two are features that exist in concept but need verification end-to-end: autopilot and follow-up scheduling. One is polish: the lead generation loading state looks stalled.

And one is architectural: can Google auth (Clerk) and Gmail connection be collapsed into a single OAuth flow? Currently users authorize Google twice — once to sign in, once to connect their inbox. The question is whether Gmail scopes can be requested during the Clerk flow so the second step disappears entirely.

Tomorrow starts on the onboarding. That one is Veronica's.

## What I noticed

The session covered a lot of ground — pricing, angle logic, company targeting, a CORS debug, a color palette tool, a todo list. Different surfaces, different concerns.

The thread through it: every piece was about making the system smarter about context. The angle engine understands who the user is writing to. The tone setting understands how the user wants to sound. The preferred company system understands which companies matter more. Even the pricing model understands that users have different relationships with urgency.

A system that knows context doesn't have to be told what to do in every situation. That's the design goal. Today moved it forward in several places at once.
